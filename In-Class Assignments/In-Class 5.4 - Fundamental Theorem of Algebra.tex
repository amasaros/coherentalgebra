\section*{Prime or Irreducible}
For each quadratic polynomial below:
\begin{clist}
\item Compute the discriminant. Based on the discriminant, determine whether the polynomial is factorable (with rational coefficients), prime (but not irreducible), or irreducible.
\item If the polynomial is not irreducible, factor using the $ac$ method, completing the square, or the quadratic formula (whichever you prefer).
\end{clist}
\begin{smenum}
\mitemxxx
{$x^2+2x-6$}
{$x^2+6x+10$}
{$x^2-2x-8$}
\mitemxxx
{$x^2-6x+4$}
{$x^2+4x+3$}
{$x^2-4x+13$}
\end{smenum}

\section*{Count of Roots}
For each polynomial below, determine the maximum number of real roots it has, up to multiplicity. (Don't try to find any of them.)
\begin{smenum}
\mitemxxx
{$2x^6-2x^3+7$}
{$-x^7+4x^4+8x^2+5$}
{$4x^6-8x^3-4$}
\mitemxx
{$5x^{12}+3x^9-3x^7+x^4+9x^2+6$}
{$6x^{11}-4x^{10}+4x^9-5x^6-3x^4+9x^2+9$}
\end{smenum}
Determine the number of real roots of each polynomial described below.
\begin{senum}
\item A polynomial of degree 7 with 1 irreducible quadratic factor.
\item A polynomial of degree 8 with no irreducible quadratic factors.
\item A polynomial of degree 6 with 3 irreducible quadratic factors.
\end{senum}
Determine the number of irreducible quadratic factors of each polynomial described below. If this can't happen, write ``impossible.''
\begin{senum}
\item A polynomial of degree 5 with 3 real roots.
\item A polynomial of degree 8 with 4 real roots.
\item A polynomial of degree 4 with 3 real roots.
\end{senum}
