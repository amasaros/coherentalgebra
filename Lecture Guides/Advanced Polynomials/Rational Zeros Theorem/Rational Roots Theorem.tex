\subsection{Rational Roots Theorem} 
\begin{theorem}[Rational Roots Theorem]
If a fraction $\frac{p}{q}$ (in lowest terms) is a root of a polynomial with \emph{integer} coefficients
$$P=\sum_{k=0}^n a_n x^n$$
then $p$ is a factor of $a_0$ (the constant term), and $q$ is a factor of $a_n$ (the leading coefficient).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
If $\frac{p}{q}$ is a root of $P$, then the factor theorem tells us that \makeblank[1in] is a factor of $P$.
\begin{itemize}
\item So $P=\makeblank[1in] Q$ for some polynomial $Q$.
\item The \makeblank[1.5in] of $P$ is \makeblank[.5in] times the leading coefficinet of $Q$
\item So \makeblank[.5in] is a factor of \makeblank[.5in]
\item Also, the \makeblank[1.5in] of $P$ is \makeblank[.5in] times the constant term of $Q$
\item So \makeblank[.5in] is a factor of \makeblank[.5in]
\end{itemize}
Looking at the coefficients give us the result we want.
\end{proof}
\begin{Example}
List all possible rational roots of the polynomial
\[
6x^5-11x^4+8x^3-9x^2+15x-15
\]
In this polynomial:
\begin{itemize}
\item $a_n=\makeblank[.5in]$, so the possible values of $q$ are \makeblank.
\item $a_0=\makeblank[.5in]$, so the possible values of $p$ are \makeblank.
\end{itemize}

\end{lpic}

\end{Example}
