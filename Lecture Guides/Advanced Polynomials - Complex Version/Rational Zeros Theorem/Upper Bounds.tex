\subsection{Upper Bounds} 
Let's try to factor the polynomial
\[
6x^3+47x^2-9x-8
\]
Its possible rational roots are
\begin{lpic}{}{5}
\foreach \y/\val in {0/1,1/2,2/3,3/6}
  \regtextleft{1}{-2-\y}{$q=\pm\val$};
\foreach \x/\val in {0/1,1/2,2/4,3/8}
  \regtext{3+\value{cols}/4-3/8+\x*\value{cols}/2-3*\x/4}{-1}{$p=\pm\val$};
\end{lpic}
The polynomial has \makeblank[.5in] sign change, so we know it has \makeblank[.5in] positive real root. We might decide to try all the positive integers first.
\begin{cpic}{}{7}
	\foreach \a in {-10,0} {
		\foreach \b in {0,-4} {
		  \synthdiv{\a}{\b}{4};
			\foreach \x/\lbl in {0/6,1/47,2/-9,3/-8}
				\regtextright{\a+2*\x+3}{\b-1}{$\lbl$};
			\regtextright{\a+3}{\b-3}{$6$};
		}
	}
	\foreach \x/\y/\lbl in {-6/-2/ 6,-4/-2/ 53,-2/-2/  44,-6/-3/53,-4/-3/ 44,-2/-3/  36,
	                         4/-2/12, 6/-2/118, 8/-2/ 218, 4/-3/59, 6/-3/109, 8/-3/ 210,
													-6/-6/24,-4/-6/284,-2/-6/1100,-6/-7/71,-4/-7/275,-2/-7/1092,
													 4/-6/48, 6/-6/760, 8/-6/6008, 4/-7/95, 6/-7/751, 8/-7/6000,
												  -10/-1/1,-10/-5/4,0/-1/2,0/-5/8}
			\regtextright{\x+1}{\y}{$\lbl$};
	\draw[dashed] (0,0) -- (0,-7);
\end{cpic}
None of them worked! It turns out that we could have known that without trying all of them.
\begin{theorem}
If we have a positive number $k$, and when performing synthetic division by $(x-k)$, every term in the quotient and the remainder have the \emph{same} sign (positive or negative), $k$ is an \emph{upper bound} on the roots of the polynomial, that is, every root of the polynomial is less than or equal to $k$.
\end{theorem}
In the example above, \makeblank[.5in] is an upper bound on the roots. So we didn't need to check \makeblank.

We should try some positive fractions (less than 1) next, or think about the negative roots.
