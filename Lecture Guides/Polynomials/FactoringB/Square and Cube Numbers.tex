\subsection{Square and Cube Numbers} 
In factoring, it can be useful to recognize certain numbers as ``perfect squares'' and ``perfect cubes,'' that is, powers of integers.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{0.9\textwidth}{|l|RRRRRRRRR|}
\hline
$x$&1&2&3&4&5&6&7&8&9\\\hline
$x^2$&1&4&9&16&25&36&49&64&81\\\hline
$x^3$&1&8&27&64&125&216&343&512&729\\\hline
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}
If you spend a lot of time factoring, you may memorize these lists. If you don't have a list like this memorized, you can use a calculator or computer to make one.
\begin{procedure}{List Squares and Cubes on the TI-84}
\begin{enumerate*}
\item Press \TIbut{y=} to access the function entry screen.
\item Enter \TI{X\TIsup{2}} as \TI{Y\TIsub{1}} and \TI{X\TIsup{3}} as \TI{Y\TIsub{2}}.
\item Press \TIbut{2nd}\ \TIbut{graph} to access the ``Table'' feature.
\item The \TI{Y\TIsub{1}} column shows perfect squares, and the \TI{Y\TIsub{2}} column shows perfect cubes. Use the up and down arrow keys to scroll through the table to see if a particular number appears.
\end{enumerate*}
\end{procedure}
\begin{Example}
Use the table above or a table generated on the calculator to identify each of the following numbers as a perfect square, a perfect cube, both, or neither.
\begin{enumerate}
\item $20$\hfill Perfect square\qquad Perfect cube\qquad Neither\makespace[1.75in]
\item $49$\hfill Perfect square\qquad Perfect cube\qquad Neither\makespace[1.75in]
\item $125$\hfill Perfect square\qquad Perfect cube\qquad Neither\makespace[1.75in]
\item $64$\hfill Perfect square\qquad Perfect cube\qquad Neither\makespace[1.75in]
\item $400$\hfill Perfect square\qquad Perfect cube\qquad Neither\makespace[1.75in]
\item $200$\hfill Perfect square\qquad Perfect cube\qquad Neither\makespace[1.75in]
\end{enumerate}
\end{Example}
