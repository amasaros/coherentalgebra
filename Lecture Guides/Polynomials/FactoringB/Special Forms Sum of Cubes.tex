\subsection{Special Forms: Sum of Cubes} 
What happens in the difference of cubes form if we replace $B$ by $-C$?
\begin{itemize*}
\item $A^3-B^3=A^3-(\makeblanksmall)^3=\makeblank$
\item $(A-B)(A^2+AB+B^2)=(A-(\makeblanksmall))(A^2+A(\makeblanksmall)+(\makeblanksmall)^2)$\lbr$=\makeblank$
\end{itemize*}
This is a \makeblank[1.5in] form, the \term{\makeblank[0.75in] of cubes}.
\begin{fact}[Recognizing a Sum of Cubes]
A binomial is a \term{sum of cubes} when:
\begin{itemize*}
\item Each term is a perfect cube monomials (\makeblank[1in] a cubed number, and powers of variables multiples of \makeblanksmall).
\item Both terms are positive.
\end{itemize*}
\end{fact}
\begin{Note} If both terms are \makeblank[1.5in], we can factor out \makeblanksmall to get a sum of cubes.
\end{Note}
\begin{Example}Factor each sum of cubes.
\begin{lpic}{}{3}
\regtextleft{0.5}{-1}{$8x^3+1={}$};
\end{lpic}
\begin{lpic}{}{3}
\regtextleft{0.5}{-1}{$x^6+y^6={}$};
\end{lpic}
\end{Example}
\begin{Note}We can generalize this for any \makeblanksmall powers (not just 3). For \makeblank[0.75in] powers, the sum is prime.\end{Note}
