\subsection{Review: Multiplying and Dividing Fractions} 
Multiplying and dividing fractions is straightforward.
\begin{procedure}{Multiplying Fractions}
To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators, then multiply the denominators.
\end{procedure}
\begin{example}Multiply $\frac{3}{8}\cdot\frac{10}{9}$. Simplify your answer as much as possible.
\begin{lpic}{}{4}
\fractextleft{0.25}{-1}{$\dfrac{3}{8}\cdot\dfrac{10}{9}={}$};
\end{lpic}
\end{example}
\begin{note}When we simplify, we must \makeblank[1in] the numerator and denominator. We didn't need to multiply \makeblank[1in] or \makeblank[1in]! In fact, we could have just factored \makeblank.
\end{note}
\begin{procedure}{Dividing Fractions}
To divide fractions, rewrite as multiplication by the reciprocal. That is, for any fractions $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}\neq 0$, $\frac{a}{b}\div\frac{c}{d}=\frac{a}{b}\cdot\frac{d}{c}$.
\end{procedure}
\begin{example}
Divide $\frac{7}{9}\div\frac{14}{15}$. Simplify your answer as much as possible.
\begin{lpic}{}{4}
\fractextleft{0.25}{-1}{$\dfrac{7}{9}\div\dfrac{14}{15}={}$};
\end{lpic}
\end{example}
